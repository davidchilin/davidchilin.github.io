Film Dialogue and Skin Color

An analysis of dialogue from 2,000 screenplays, compared by skin color.

(NOTE: This is a replica of Pudding.cool's gender & film dialogue analysis with a new dataset on skin color. Visualization and theme/style is based on pieces by Matt Daniels, Hanah Anderson, and Champe Barton. The Pudding/Barton had no involvement in the development or publication of this specific project.)

In Hollywood, who gets to speak? We know that dialogue often skews toward male characters, but what about representation across the spectrum of skin lightness?
Does the amount of dialogue an actor gets correlate with the lightness of their skin?
This project analyzes dialogue from over 2,000 films to find out, using a dataset that quantifies skin lightness for over 8,600 actors.

*Domestic gross over $45M, inflation-adjusted. Using IMDB box office, 2,500 have hit this threshold.

Methodology

Daniels and Anderson mapped characters with at least 100 words of dialogue in each of the 2,000 screenplays in their database to the respective actors’ IMDB pages. Any character who failed to breach this 100 word threshold was ignored, so some films with 100% white or nonwhite dialogue may in reality contain <100% one way or the other.

*Skin lightness was quantified by processing each actor’s IMDB photo through a face recognition filter. These faces were passed through a color map filter which processed the most likely face colors as skin and provides an average RGB (Red, Gree, Blue) value. In order to compare the skin colors, RGB values were translated to HLS (Hue, Saturation, Lightness) and Lightness was used as the main comparision metric.

Not all characters are present as some don't have IMDB profile photos, and if they do, the photo is black & white or the face recognition filter did not detect a face. This led to a reduction in characters used from Pudding's 23,000 to this projects 8,600 faces.

Career Arcs: Comparing Actor Age by Skin Color

The data also reveals a stark difference in career longevity. For white actors, dialogue peaks in their 40s and gradually declines. For nonwhite actors, however, the peak is much earlier—in their 30s—and the drop-off is significantly steeper.
